# GDV Evaluation Analysis

## Purpose

The GDV evaluation checked whether the static figures communicate the intended football insights clearly. The focus was not on statistical proof, but on formative feedback for improving the visual communication.

## Method

A small think-aloud evaluation was conducted with three football-interested participants. The participants looked at the final GDV figures and answered five interpretation tasks. Afterwards, they gave short feedback about which figure was easiest to understand, which figure was most confusing and what should be improved.

This method was chosen because the GDV project focuses on whether viewers can correctly interpret the static visualizations. A think-aloud procedure is suitable for detecting misunderstandings in labels, legends, chart structure and visual explanations.

## Participants

Three participants were used. They had basic football knowledge and were able to interpret common chart types such as bar charts and scatterplots.

The evaluation was anonymised with participant IDs P1, P2 and P3.

## Tasks

The participants answered five tasks:

1. Identify the most common goal build-up type.
2. Interpret the relationship between passes before goal and attack duration.
3. Name one direct and one patient team.
4. Interpret the finishing efficiency vs tournament progress figure without making a causal claim.
5. Explain the Spain case study sequence.

## Task results

All three participants answered all five tasks correctly.

| Task | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Task 1 | 3/3 correct | The build-up type chart communicated the main pattern clearly. |
| Task 2 | 3/3 correct | The scatterplot was understandable and participants noticed the positive relationship with some variation. |
| Task 3 | 3/3 correct | The direct-vs-patient team comparison was clear enough for team-level interpretation. |
| Task 4 | 3/3 correct | Participants understood that efficiency and tournament progress should not be interpreted causally. |
| Task 5 | 3/3 correct | The Spain case study was understandable after the numbered sequence was explained. |

## Qualitative feedback

The easiest figures were the build-up type chart, the direct-vs-patient team comparison and the Spain case study. Participants could quickly identify that quick attacks were the most common build-up type.

The most confusing figures were the efficiency vs tournament progress figure, the Spain case study and the stacked build-up type chart. The main problems were not the data itself, but the explanations around the figures.

Main feedback themes:

1. The efficiency chart could be misunderstood as causal.
2. The Spain case study needed a short explanation of the numbered pass order.
3. The categories Quick, Medium and Long needed to be defined before interpreting the stacked chart.

## Design improvements made

Based on the evaluation, the report and figure explanations were revised:

1. The efficiency figure is now described as an association, not a causal relationship.
2. The Spain case study explanation now states that the numbers show the order of completed passes before the goal.
3. The build-up categories are defined before the corresponding charts are interpreted.

## Conclusion

The evaluation showed that the main GDV visualizations are understandable. The figures communicated the central findings, especially that quick attacks were the most common goal build-up type. The most important improvements were textual clarifications around the figures, because the main risks were misinterpretation of causality and unclear category definitions.
